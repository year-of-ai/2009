# Gaza War and Ceasefire (2008–2009)

**Category**: History & Politics
**Key figures**: Ehud Olmert (Israeli PM during operation), Tzipi Livni (Israeli Foreign Minister), Ehud Barak (Israeli Defense Minister), Benjamin Netanyahu (won February 2009 elections), Mahmoud Abbas (Palestinian Authority President), Ismail Haniyeh (Hamas Prime Minister, Gaza), Khaled Mashal (Hamas political bureau chief, Damascus), Ban Ki-moon (UN Secretary-General), Richard Goldstone (UN Fact-Finding Mission chair), Recep Tayyip Erdoğan (Turkish PM, leading international critic)

## Summary

The Gaza War — codenamed "Operation Cast Lead" by Israel — was a 22-day military offensive launched on December 27, 2008 and concluded with a unilateral ceasefire on January 18, 2009. Triggered by the breakdown of a six-month Egyptian-brokered truce between Israel and Hamas and the resumption of rocket fire from Gaza, the operation combined the most intensive Israeli air campaign in decades with a ground invasion into densely populated urban terrain. The war's cessation, and the prolonged humanitarian and political reckoning it set in motion, defined the opening months of 2009.

## The Military Campaign

Israel launched the air campaign on the morning of December 27, 2008, striking approximately 100 targets simultaneously — among them Hamas police headquarters, government ministries, training facilities, and storage sites for rockets and weapons caches. The opening strikes killed some 230 people in a single day, the highest single-day toll of the conflict. Over the subsequent week-long air offensive (December 27–January 3), Israeli aircraft conducted an estimated 2,400 airstrikes, targeting launch pads, tunnel networks along the Egypt–Gaza border, and the homes of senior Hamas commanders.

A land invasion began on the night of January 3–4, 2009, when Israeli armored and infantry units entered northern Gaza. Ground forces pushed into densely built-up neighborhoods of Jabaliya, Beit Lahiya, and Beit Hanoun. Fighting was sustained and brutal: Hamas fighters employed improvised explosive devices, booby-trapped buildings, and narrow-alley ambushes designed to neutralize Israeli armor. Israeli forces nonetheless advanced, encircling Gaza City by January 14. A second axis opened in the south around Khan Yunis. Israeli combat operations ceased at 2:00 a.m. on January 18, 2009, when Olmert declared a unilateral ceasefire. Hamas announced a separate one-week ceasefire hours later; both sides began withdrawing to pre-invasion positions over the following days.

The toll was severe and contested. Israeli human-rights organization B'Tselem documented 1,387 Palestinian deaths, including 773 civilians (56% of total). Palestinian sources put civilian deaths at 60–70%. The Israeli military maintained that only 295 of the dead were non-combatant civilians. Israel suffered 13 fatalities — 10 soldiers and 3 civilians. Over 400,000 people in Gaza were left without reliable access to running water; 4,000 homes were destroyed outright, with an estimated 16,000 damaged. The UN Relief and Works Agency (UNRWA) reported that 50,800 Gazans were displaced within the strip by mid-January.

Prior to the operation, Gaza-based militant groups — primarily Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad — had fired some 3,000 rockets into southern Israel in 2008, killing four Israeli civilians and disrupting daily life in towns like Sderot, Ashkelon, and Ashdod.

## International Response and UN Resolution 1860

The international reaction was swift and largely condemnatory of Israeli tactics, though divided on the underlying justifications. On January 8, 2009 — ten days into the operation — the UN Security Council passed Resolution 1860, calling for an "immediate, durable and fully respected ceasefire" and demanding unimpeded humanitarian access to Gaza. The resolution passed 14-0, with the United States abstaining. Israel rejected the resolution as premature; Hamas dismissed it as non-binding without their consent.

Turkey's Prime Minister Erdoğan — whose country had previously served as a mediator between Israel and Syria — delivered perhaps the most dramatic public rebuke. At the World Economic Forum in Davos on January 29, 2009, Erdoğan confronted Israeli President Shimon Peres directly, accusing Israel of committing crimes against humanity and storming off stage. The episode marked the beginning of a deep rupture in Israeli-Turkish relations.

The UN Human Rights Council convened an emergency session on January 9, 2009, and voted 33–1 (with 13 abstentions) to condemn Israel's "grave violations" of international law. The council also commissioned a formal fact-finding mission, to be led by South African jurist Richard Goldstone.

## The Goldstone Report

In September 2009, the UN Fact-Finding Mission on the Gaza Conflict released its 574-page report — immediately dubbed the "Goldstone Report." It documented evidence of potential war crimes and possible crimes against humanity by both Israeli forces and Hamas. Against Israel, the report cited deliberate targeting of civilians and civilian infrastructure (including UNRWA schools used as shelters), use of white phosphorus munitions over populated areas, and collective punishment. Against Hamas, it cited deliberate and indiscriminate rocket fire at Israeli civilian centers.

The report sparked intense controversy. Israel rejected it outright as biased and based on flawed testimony; the United States, allied with Israel, opposed its findings in the UN General Assembly. The Palestinian Authority initially supported it but faced pressure to delay a vote; Hamas welcomed findings against Israel but ignored those directed at itself. The report was debated at the UN General Assembly in November 2009 and ultimately referred to the Security Council, where no further action was taken.

In 2011, Goldstone himself published a partial retraction in a Washington Post op-ed, stating that new evidence suggested Israel had not deliberately targeted civilians as a policy — though co-signatories to the report stood by its original findings. The controversy over the report illustrated the profound difficulty of adjudicating military conduct in asymmetric urban warfare.

## Political Aftermath: Israeli Elections and Regional Diplomacy

The operation concluded in the final days of the Olmert government. Parliamentary elections held February 10, 2009 produced a fractured Knesset, but Likud leader Benjamin Netanyahu — who had campaigned on a security-hardline platform opposing any two-state compromise — ultimately assembled a right-wing coalition and took office as Prime Minister on March 31, 2009. His government included the nationalist Yisrael Beiteinu party (led by Avigdor Lieberman, who became Foreign Minister) and ultra-Orthodox parties, representing a notable rightward shift in Israeli politics.

The new government immediately complicated diplomatic outreach from the newly inaugurated Obama administration. President Obama, who was sworn in on January 20, 2009 — two days after the ceasefire — dispatched special envoy George Mitchell to the region in January. Obama's April 2009 Cairo speech to the Muslim world and his September Nobel Peace Prize acceptance (see [Obama Nobel Peace Prize](obama-nobel-peace-prize-2009.md)) explicitly invoked the Israeli-Palestinian conflict as central to US foreign policy ambitions. However, Netanyahu's refusal to freeze settlement construction blocked resumption of formal peace talks.

The war consolidated Hamas's internal political position in Gaza. The Palestinian Authority under Mahmoud Abbas, which had cooperated with Israel during Operation Cast Lead, suffered a devastating loss of credibility among Palestinians. Hamas, despite heavy material losses, emerged from the war politically stronger — having fought the Israeli military to a standstill and survived, in contrast to the PA's passive posture. Palestinian reconciliation talks between Hamas and Fatah, which might have yielded a unified Palestinian delegation for negotiations, remained stalled throughout 2009.

## Humanitarian Crisis and the Blockade

The cessation of active combat did not end Gaza's humanitarian emergency. The blockade on the Gaza Strip — in place since Hamas seized control of the enclave in June 2007 — remained tightly enforced after the ceasefire, restricting the import of construction materials, fuel, and certain food items. Israel classified cement, steel rebar, and glass as dual-use materials that could be repurposed for military construction, and kept them off the permitted goods list. As a result, reconstruction of the estimated 4,000 destroyed homes and 16,000 damaged structures proceeded at negligible pace through 2009.

The World Food Programme reported in mid-2009 that 61% of Gaza's population of 1.5 million was "food insecure," dependent on UN food aid to meet minimum nutritional needs. UNICEF documented elevated rates of anemia among children under five. The Egyptian border crossing at Rafah, which could have served as an alternative supply route, was kept largely closed under Egyptian pressure — Cairo feared that open borders would strengthen Hamas and inflame its own internal Islamist opposition.

Médecins Sans Frontières and the International Committee of the Red Cross submitted joint reports in 2009 documenting the medical system's collapse: hospitals lacked surgical supplies, anesthetics, and replacement parts for imaging equipment. The sole power plant in Gaza operated at 30–45% capacity, causing rolling blackouts of 8–12 hours daily, affecting hospital generators, water pumps, and refrigeration for medicines.

## Significance

The Gaza War defined the opening weeks of 2009 and left a durable political and legal legacy. Domestically, it closed out the Olmert government and helped return Benjamin Netanyahu to power at the head of a right-wing coalition, marking a lasting rightward shift in Israeli politics that complicated the incoming Obama administration's peace overtures. Diplomatically, it triggered the rupture in Israeli–Turkish relations dramatized by the Erdoğan–Peres confrontation at Davos, and it strengthened Hamas's standing in Gaza relative to Mahmoud Abbas's Palestinian Authority. The Goldstone Report — and Richard Goldstone's later partial retraction — became a landmark reference in the international debate over accountability for military conduct in asymmetric urban warfare. The continued blockade and stalled reconstruction entrenched a humanitarian crisis that shaped Gaza's trajectory well beyond 2009.
