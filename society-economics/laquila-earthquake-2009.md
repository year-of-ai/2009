# 2009 L'Aquila Earthquake

**Category**: Society & Economics
**Key figures**: Silvio Berlusconi (Prime Minister of Italy, ordered the G8 summit relocated to L'Aquila), Guido Bertolaso (head of Italy's Civil Protection Department)

## Summary

At 03:32 local time (01:32 UTC) on April 6, 2009, an earthquake measuring magnitude 6.3 on the moment magnitude scale (roughly 5.8–5.9 on the Richter scale) struck the Abruzzo region of central Italy, centered near the city of L'Aquila, about 90 kilometers northeast of Rome. The quake followed weeks of smaller foreshocks that had unsettled residents but had not prompted an evacuation. It killed 308 people, making it Italy's deadliest earthquake since the 1980 Irpinia earthquake, injured well over a thousand more, and left an estimated 65,000–66,000 people homeless.

The historic center of L'Aquila, a medieval city, sustained extensive damage: between roughly 3,000 and 11,000 buildings were damaged or destroyed, including the Basilica of San Bernardino, the Basilica of Santa Maria di Collemaggio, the city's cathedral, and the state archives. Much of the old town was cordoned off and remained largely uninhabitable for years during a slow, heavily criticized reconstruction effort. In response, Italian Prime Minister Silvio Berlusconi moved the venue of the already-scheduled 35th G8 summit from the Sardinian resort island of La Maddalena to L'Aquila, where it was held July 8–10, 2009, a gesture intended to direct international attention and reconstruction funding toward the stricken city.

The disaster also produced a protracted legal reckoning over risk communication. In 2012 — after the year covered by this entry — an Italian court convicted six scientists and a government official of manslaughter for allegedly having provided a falsely reassuring risk assessment days before the quake, sentencing each to six years in prison; the case drew international criticism from the scientific community, and on appeal in November 2014 the six scientists were acquitted while the government official's conviction was upheld with a reduced sentence — an outcome the Italian Supreme Court confirmed in November 2015.

## Significance

The L'Aquila earthquake was one of the deadliest natural disasters in Italy in decades and exposed longstanding weaknesses in the country's seismic building codes, historic-preservation practices, and disaster-recovery bureaucracy, given the concentration of casualties in unreinforced older buildings and even some student housing. Relocating the G8 summit to the damaged city placed the disaster and Italy's earthquake preparedness before a global audience within months of the event. The subsequent criminal case against members of the National Commission for the Forecast and Prevention of Major Risks, whose convictions were later overturned on appeal, became an internationally cited cautionary episode in the debate over how scientists should communicate seismic hazard and uncertainty to the public.
