# The Beatles 09/09/09 Remasters and Rock Band

**Category** Arts & Culture  
**Key figures** Paul McCartney, Ringo Starr, Yoko Ono and Olivia Harrison (Apple Corps / estate representatives), Allan Rouse and Guy Massey (EMI remastering engineers, Abbey Road Studios), Dhani Harrison (initiator of the *Rock Band* project), Harmonix and MTV Games (*The Beatles: Rock Band* developer/publisher)

## Summary

On September 9, 2009 — a date rendered as 09/09/09 and chosen partly for the number nine's long association with John Lennon — Apple Corps and EMI released the entire Beatles studio catalogue in newly remastered form, the most comprehensive sonic overhaul of the recordings since their 1987 transition to compact disc. The release comprised individually remastered stereo albums, a *Beatles Stereo Box Set* (*The Beatles: The Original Studio Recordings*), and a separate *The Beatles in Mono* box collecting the monaural mixes as the band and their producers originally approved them. The same day, Harmonix and MTV Games released *The Beatles: Rock Band*, a music video game built around the catalogue. The remastered CDs sold approximately 2.25 million copies in their first five days across North America, Japan, and the United Kingdom, and the Beatles placed sixteen titles on *Billboard*'s Top Pop Catalog chart. The Beatles' studio recordings would not become available for digital download on the iTunes Store until more than a year later, on November 16, 2010.

## Background: The 1987 CD masters and their limitations

The Beatles' catalogue had first appeared on compact disc in 1987, in transfers supervised by original producer George Martin. Those masters, produced in the early years of CD, were widely regarded by later engineers and audiophiles as sonically dated, with the first four albums issued only in mono and the remainder in versions that did not reflect advances in digital transfer and noise-reduction technology. By the mid-2000s, Apple Corps and EMI had begun a multi-year remastering programme at Abbey Road Studios, using the original analogue master tapes as sources.

The project was led by EMI senior studio engineers Allan Rouse and Guy Massey, with a team applying 24-bit digital transfer, careful de-noising, and equalisation intended to preserve rather than modernise the original sound. The stereo remasters were prepared first; a parallel effort assembled the mono mixes, which for albums through *The Beatles* (the "White Album") had been the versions the band actively supervised in the 1960s, when stereo was still a secondary consumer format.

## The September 9, 2009 releases

The 09/09/09 rollout had three coordinated components:

- **Remastered stereo albums**: All of the Beatles' core studio albums, plus the two-volume *Past Masters* compilation of non-album singles and B-sides, were reissued individually with new packaging, restored artwork, and short documentary films embedded on each disc.
- **The Beatles Stereo Box Set** (*The Beatles: The Original Studio Recordings*): A boxed set gathering the full remastered stereo catalogue across sixteen discs, including a DVD collecting the mini-documentaries.
- **The Beatles in Mono**: A separate boxed set collecting the remastered monaural recordings, aimed at listeners and collectors who regarded the mono mixes as the definitive versions of the 1960s albums.

*The Beatles: Rock Band*, developed by Harmonix and published by MTV Games and Electronic Arts, shipped the same day. The game — whose development had been encouraged by Dhani Harrison, son of George Harrison, and approved by the surviving members and estates — set career mode against a stylised journey through the band's career, from the Cavern Club to Abbey Road, and marked the first time the Beatles' master recordings had been licensed for a video game.

## Commercial reception

The remasters were a substantial commercial success. Across their first five days on sale, the remastered CDs sold roughly 2.25 million copies in North America, Japan, and the United Kingdom combined. On *Billboard*'s Top Pop Catalog chart, the Beatles placed sixteen titles in the top fifty in the weeks following release, including the fourteen individually remastered CDs and both box sets — an unusually dominant showing for a single act on a catalogue chart.

*The Beatles: Rock Band* performed more modestly relative to pre-release expectations. It sold approximately 583,000 units in the United States in September 2009, below some analyst forecasts for a title carrying the Beatles brand, though it outsold its principal competitor, Activision's *Guitar Hero 5*, in the same month. The game's reception nonetheless helped introduce the catalogue to younger audiences and demonstrated the appetite for licensed music-based gaming at the format's commercial peak.

## The digital hold-out and the 2010 iTunes release

Notably absent from the 09/09/09 event was any digital download release. The Beatles remained one of the most prominent major-artist hold-outs from digital music sales, reflecting both the surviving members' and estate's concerns about audio quality in compressed formats and an unresolved commercial and trademark history between Apple Corps and Apple Inc. (whose disputes over the "Apple" name had run from 1978 to a 2007 settlement). The catalogue did not reach the iTunes Store until November 16, 2010 — more than a year after the remasters — when Apple Corps, EMI, and Apple Inc. jointly announced its digital availability worldwide.

## Significance

The 09/09/09 remasters represented the most thorough restoration of the Beatles' recorded legacy in more than two decades and reset the definitive digital-era editions of the catalogue for a new generation of listeners. By pairing the stereo reissues with a dedicated *The Beatles in Mono* set, the release also elevated the standing of the mono mixes among critics and collectors and reframed a scholarly debate about which versions best represented the band's original intentions. Coordinated with *The Beatles: Rock Band*, the campaign extended the catalogue into interactive media at the height of the music-game boom. The event underscored how carefully the surviving Beatles and their estates managed the band's commercial afterlife — releasing on their own terms in high-quality physical formats while continuing, for another fourteen months, to withhold their music from digital download.
