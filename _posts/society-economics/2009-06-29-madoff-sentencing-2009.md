## Summary

On 29 June 2009, financier Bernard "Bernie" Madoff was sentenced in the US District Court for the Southern District of New York to 150 years in federal prison — the maximum allowable term — for orchestrating the largest Ponzi scheme in history. Madoff had pleaded guilty on 12 March 2009 to 11 federal felony counts, including securities fraud, wire fraud, mail fraud, money laundering, and perjury. Judge Denny Chin described the fraud as "extraordinarily evil" and imposed the symbolic maximum, rejecting a defense request for a sentence of about 12 years.

## The Scheme

For decades, Madoff operated a wealth-management business that purported to generate steady, above-market returns through a "split-strike conversion" strategy. In reality, no genuine investing took place: client withdrawals and fictitious profits were paid out of other investors' deposits, the defining structure of a Ponzi scheme. The fraud unraveled in December 2008 amid the global financial crisis, when a wave of redemption requests left Madoff unable to meet withdrawals, and he confessed to his sons, who reported him to authorities. He was arrested on 11 December 2008.

Prosecutors placed the nominal size of the fraud at roughly $64.8 billion, the total reflected in the fabricated account statements of about 4,800 client accounts as of late November 2008. Actual cash losses of investor principal were later estimated in the range of $17–20 billion. The collapse devastated individual investors, charities, university endowments, and feeder funds worldwide.

## Aftermath

A court-appointed trustee, Irving Picard, pursued an extensive, multi-year recovery effort through "clawback" litigation against parties who had withdrawn more than they invested, eventually recovering billions of dollars for victims. Several associates and family members faced legal and financial consequences in the years that followed. Madoff died in federal prison on 14 April 2021 at age 82.

## Significance

The Madoff affair became the defining white-collar fraud of the Great Recession era. It exposed severe failures of regulatory oversight — the US Securities and Exchange Commission had received and failed to act on credible warnings for years — and prompted reforms in investor protection, auditing of investment advisers, and due-diligence practices. The case stands as a touchstone in discussions of financial trust, the limits of regulation, and the human cost of large-scale fraud.
