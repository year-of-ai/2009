# Kanye West Interrupts Taylor Swift at MTV VMAs

**Category**: People  
**Key figures**: Kanye West, Taylor Swift, Beyoncé, Russell Brand, Barack Obama, Jay Leno

## Summary

On September 13, 2009, at the 2009 MTV Video Music Awards (VMAs) held at Radio City Music Hall in New York City — hosted by British comedian Russell Brand — rapper Kanye West interrupted 19-year-old Taylor Swift's acceptance speech for Best Female Video to declare that Beyoncé had made "one of the best videos of all time." Swift was receiving the award for "You Belong with Me," marking her first-ever VMA win, when West walked onstage uninvited, took the microphone from her hand, and said: *"Yo, Taylor, I'm really happy for you, I'mma let you finish, but Beyoncé had one of the best videos of all time!"* — referring to Beyoncé's "Single Ladies (Put a Ring on It)." The arena fell mostly silent; Swift stood on stage visibly stunned as West held the microphone aloft and was then escorted off. Beyoncé's "Single Ladies" went on to win Video of the Year later that evening.

West had arrived at the VMAs with four nominations — "Heartless," "Love Lockdown," "Amazing," and "Paranoid" — all of which lost. His abrupt takeover of Swift's first major televised acceptance speech became one of the most-replayed moments in awards-show television history.

## The Night's Timeline

- **Pre-show**: West attended a pre-show party and was reported by multiple witnesses to have been drinking from a bottle of Hennessy cognac before entering the venue.
- **Best Female Video announcement**: Swift won over Beyoncé ("Single Ladies"), Lady Gaga ("Poker Face"), Katy Perry ("Hot N Cold"), and Pink ("So What").
- **West's interruption**: Within seconds of Swift beginning her speech, West mounted the stage, praised Beyoncé's video, and was led away as the audience booed. Swift did not complete her speech.
- **Backstage**: Photographs circulated showing Swift appearing tearful backstage; West was reportedly ejected from the venue. Beyoncé, also backstage, was described by attendees as shocked and apologetic toward Swift.
- **Later that night**: When Beyoncé won Video of the Year for "Single Ladies," she invited Swift back to the stage to finish the speech West had cut short — a gesture widely praised as gracious and restorative.

## Immediate Aftermath

Reaction was swift and overwhelmingly negative. During an off-the-record portion of a pre-interview for CNBC on September 14, President Barack Obama — in his first year in office — called West a "jackass." The comment was inadvertently published by ABC News reporter Terry Moran on Twitter before being deleted, but the remark spread rapidly and was later confirmed by the White House. West appeared on the debut episode of *The Jay Leno Show* on September 14 (the day after the VMAs) and struggled visibly to explain his actions, ultimately deflecting with references to the recent death of his mother, Donda West, in November 2007. Leno's on-camera question — "What do you think [your mother] would have said about this?" — left West momentarily speechless.

West posted a written apology on his blog that same evening, later deleted. He issued a subsequent public apology to Swift via Twitter in 2010.

## Long-Term Cultural Impact

The incident ignited a multi-year public narrative pitting West and Swift against each other that resurfaced repeatedly throughout the following decade:

- **2010**: Swift performed "Innocent" at the 2010 VMAs, widely interpreted as a letter to West, with lyrics including *"you're still an innocent."*
- **2015**: The two appeared to reconcile at the August 2015 VMAs, where Swift presented West with the Michael Jackson Video Vanguard Award.
- **2016**: West released "Famous," which referenced Swift in a sexually explicit lyric he claimed she had approved. Swift denied this; a phone call recording later surfaced that was disputed by both sides, reigniting global media coverage.

Rolling Stone named the interruption the wildest moment in VMA history in a 2013 retrospective. The phrase "Imma let you finish" became an instant internet meme, generating hundreds of parody images, remixed videos, and late-night comedy sketches within 48 hours and entering popular culture vocabulary as shorthand for rude interruption. The moment also accelerated the careers of both principals: Swift's *Fearless* re-entered the sales charts within days of the incident, and West's notoriety — already substantial — reached a new global scale.

## Significance

The VMA incident crystallized the 2009 media climate in which celebrity behavior, real-time social commentary (Twitter was two years old), and political response could combine into a cultural flashpoint within hours. It prefigured the era of social-media-driven celebrity scandal, with Obama's "jackass" comment demonstrating how digital communication could inadvertently broadcast private reactions to a global audience. For Swift, the moment of public humiliation transformed her from a country-crossover artist into a broadly sympathized mainstream figure; for West, it became the defining controversy of his career up to that point — eclipsing even his Grammy-season prominence earlier in 2009. Beyoncé's decision to invite Swift back onstage became equally discussed as a counterexample of grace under pressure.
