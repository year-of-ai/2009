# Uyghur Unrest in Xinjiang

**Category:** Society & Economics | **Date:** July 5, 2009
**Key figures:** Rebiya Kadeer (World Uyghur Congress president, blamed by Beijing); Wang Lequan (Communist Party Secretary of Xinjiang 2009); Nur Bekri (Chairman of Xinjiang Autonomous Region)

## Summary

On July 5, 2009, deadly ethnic riots erupted in Ürümqi, the capital of the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region in northwestern China. The violence left at least **197 people dead** and over **1,700 injured** by official Chinese government figures — making it the deadliest episode of civil unrest in China since the 1989 Tiananmen Square crackdown. Triggered by the killings of Uyghur migrant workers in Guangdong Province nine days earlier, the riots crystallized decades of accumulated grievances between the Uyghur ethnic minority and Han Chinese majority, and drew sustained international attention to Beijing's governance of Xinjiang.

## Background: Xinjiang and Uyghur-Han Relations

Xinjiang (officially the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region) covers approximately 1.66 million square kilometres in northwestern China — about one-sixth of the country's total land area — and borders eight countries. The Uyghurs, a Turkic-speaking, predominantly Muslim people, are the region's largest indigenous ethnic group, constituting roughly **45% of Xinjiang's population of 21 million** in 2009, with Han Chinese comprising approximately **40%** (up from around 6% in 1949, reflecting decades of state-encouraged Han migration).

Uyghur grievances in 2009 centered on several structural conditions:

- **Economic marginalization:** Han Chinese migrants disproportionately occupied skilled and managerial positions in Xinjiang's oil, mining, and construction sectors, while Uyghurs were concentrated in lower-wage agricultural and informal labor.
- **Cultural and religious restrictions:** The Chinese state limited expressions of Islamic practice, including restrictions on fasting during Ramadan for government employees and students, and regulated Uyghur-language education in favor of Mandarin.
- **Security apparatus:** A heavy police and paramilitary presence — justified by Beijing on anti-separatism and counter-terrorism grounds — was experienced by Uyghur communities as collective punishment.
- **Demographic pressure:** State-sponsored Han migration under the "Open Up the West" (西部大开发) campaign, begun in 2000, accelerated economic development while altering Xinjiang's ethnic composition.

## The Shaoguan Incident and Trigger Events

The immediate precipitant of the July riots was a violent brawl at **the Xuri Toy Factory in Shaoguan, Guangdong Province, on June 26, 2009**. A false rumor — later attributed to online posts — claimed that Uyghur workers had sexually assaulted Han women at the factory. Han co-workers attacked Uyghur dormitories; in the melee, **at least two Uyghurs were killed** and dozens injured. Chinese authorities arrested several individuals but Uyghur advocacy groups contested the casualty figures and the official account of the trigger.

News and photographs of the Shaoguan attack spread rapidly through Uyghur diaspora networks and within Xinjiang, amplified by early social media platforms. Rebiya Kadeer, president of the World Uyghur Congress and a prominent exile leader whom Beijing designated a separatist agitator, called for demonstrations in Ürümqi.

## Events of July 5 and the Crackdown

On the evening of July 5, approximately **1,000 demonstrators** gathered in Ürümqi's People's Square, initially calling for an investigation into the Shaoguan deaths. When police moved to disperse the crowd, protesters attacked vehicles, storefronts, and passersby. Violence spread across Uyghur and Han-mixed neighborhoods; rioters set fires, overturned buses, and attacked individuals on ethnic lines. Chinese security forces — police, People's Armed Police (PAP), and eventually military units — deployed in force.

A secondary wave of violence occurred on July 6–7, when groups of Han vigilantes armed with clubs, pipes, and knives moved through Uyghur neighborhoods in apparent reprisal. Government forces eventually suppressed all unrest by July 8.

Key enforcement measures in the aftermath:

- **Internet and mobile service blackout:** All internet access and most mobile phone services in Xinjiang were cut off from July 5, 2009, and remained blocked for approximately **10 months**, restored only in May 2010 — one of the longest communications blackouts in a major region of a modern state.
- **Mass arrests:** Chinese authorities reported arresting approximately **1,500 people**; independent observers believed the figure was higher and that many were held without charge.
- **Show trials:** By late 2009, nine people had been sentenced to death and executed for riot-related offenses; human rights organizations raised due-process concerns.

## International Response

The July 2009 riots generated immediate international condemnation and diplomatic friction:

- **United States:** The State Department expressed concern over the violence and called for restraint; Rebiya Kadeer, a U.S. resident, was invited to testify before the Congressional-Executive Commission on China.
- **Turkey:** Prime Minister Erdoğan characterized the Chinese response as "a kind of genocide," prompting a sharp diplomatic rebuke from Beijing and a brief strain in Sino-Turkish relations.
- **European Union:** Issued statements calling for dialogue and the release of detainees held without charge.
- **Organization of Islamic Cooperation (OIC):** Issued a statement of concern, though stopped short of formal censure of China.

China's government maintained that outside forces — chiefly the World Uyghur Congress and foreign governments — had instigated the unrest, rejecting international criticism as interference in internal affairs. This framing would persist and intensify in subsequent years.

## Significance

The July 2009 riots marked a watershed in both the internal governance of Xinjiang and in global awareness of Uyghur-Han tensions. In the immediate term, Beijing doubled down on the "stability maintenance" (维稳) security framework: surveillance infrastructure was expanded, checkpoints multiplied, and restrictions on religious and cultural expression tightened.

In the longer arc, 2009 served as a critical precursor to the mass detention policies of the 2017–2020 period, when international investigators documented what the UN described as credible evidence of more than **one million Uyghurs and members of other Muslim minorities** held in extrajudicial detention facilities. Multiple governments and legislatures — including the United States, Canada, the Netherlands, and the United Kingdom — later characterized these policies as genocide or crimes against humanity, positions China consistently rejected.

The internet blackout imposed after July 5 also foreshadowed the development of China's domestic "Great Firewall" and information-control capabilities that became hallmarks of the country's digital governance model into the 2020s.

The 2009 unrest coincided with a particularly tumultuous year in Chinese diplomacy: the same year saw intense negotiations over climate policy at the [Copenhagen Climate Conference](../history-politics/cop15-copenhagen-2009.md) and the [Iran Green Movement](../history-politics/iran-green-movement-2009.md) protests — both instances where authoritarian governments faced international scrutiny over treatment of internal dissent.
