## Summary

On August 6, 2009, the United States Senate confirmed Sonia Sotomayor as an Associate Justice of the Supreme Court by a vote of 68–31, making her the first Hispanic and only the third woman to serve on the Court. President Barack Obama had announced her nomination on May 26, 2009, to fill the seat being vacated by Associate Justice David Souter, who had announced his retirement earlier that spring. It was the first Supreme Court appointment of Obama's presidency.

Sotomayor's path to the Court was extensively documented. Born in the Bronx, New York, on June 25, 1954, to Puerto Rican parents, she earned a bachelor's degree from Princeton University in 1976 and a law degree from Yale Law School in 1979. She served as an assistant district attorney in Manhattan, entered private practice, and was appointed a federal trial judge on the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York in 1992 before her elevation to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit in 1998. At the time of her nomination she had more federal judicial experience than any incoming justice in seven decades.

The Senate Judiciary Committee held confirmation hearings from July 13 to July 16, 2009, during which Sotomayor faced questioning over judicial temperament and over a widely cited 2001 speech in which she had referred to the perspective of a "wise Latina." The committee reported her nomination favorably on July 28 by a vote of 13–6, with one Republican, Lindsey Graham, joining the Democratic majority. Nine Republicans ultimately joined all present Democrats and independents in the full Senate vote. She took the judicial and constitutional oaths of office on August 8, 2009, administered by Chief Justice John Roberts.

## Significance

Sotomayor's confirmation was a landmark in the representation of Hispanic Americans in the federal government, placing the first Latina justice on the nation's highest court. Her elevation also increased the number of women serving simultaneously on the Court and, together with the appointment of Elena Kagan the following year, contributed to a Court that for the first time would include three female justices.

The confirmation came at a moment when the Obama administration, inaugurated in January 2009, was reshaping the federal judiciary alongside its legislative agenda. As Obama's first appointment to the Supreme Court, it offered an early test of the new president's nomination strategy and of the Senate confirmation process in a closely divided political environment. The relatively comfortable margin — securing votes from members of both parties — reflected both Sotomayor's lengthy judicial record and the Democratic majority in the Senate.

In the broader narrative of 2009, the confirmation stood alongside other milestones of Obama's first year in office, marking a durable institutional change whose effects would extend far beyond the calendar year. Sotomayor went on to become an influential voice on the Court, but the events of mid-2009 are remembered chiefly for breaking a barrier that had stood since the Court's founding in 1789.
