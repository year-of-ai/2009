## Summary

At the 81st Academy Awards, held on February 22, 2009, at the Kodak Theatre in Hollywood, the British drama *Slumdog Millionaire* won eight Oscars, including Best Picture and Best Director for Danny Boyle. Nominated in ten categories, it converted eight of those nominations — among the strongest single-night performances of the decade — and became one of the rare Best Picture winners to triumph without a single acting nomination. The ceremony, hosted by actor Hugh Jackman, marked an unusual moment in which a modestly budgeted film set and largely shot in India dominated the industry's highest honors.

Directed by Boyle and co-directed in India by Loveleen Tandan, *Slumdog Millionaire* tells the story of Jamal Malik, a young man from the Mumbai slums whose appearance on the Indian version of *Who Wants to Be a Millionaire?* frames a series of flashbacks through his life. Adapted by Simon Beaufoy from Vikas Swarup's novel *Q & A*, the film was made on a budget of roughly $15 million and went on to gross more than $370 million worldwide.

## The Awards

*Slumdog Millionaire*'s eight wins were:

- **Best Picture**
- **Best Director** — Danny Boyle
- **Best Adapted Screenplay** — Simon Beaufoy
- **Best Cinematography** — Anthony Dod Mantle
- **Best Film Editing** — Chris Dickens
- **Best Original Score** — A. R. Rahman
- **Best Original Song** — "Jai Ho", music by A. R. Rahman, lyrics by Gulzar
- **Best Sound Mixing** — Ian Tapp, Richard Pryke, and Resul Pookutty

A. R. Rahman was the night's standout individual winner, taking home two Oscars; he had two of the three Best Original Song nominees, with "Jai Ho" winning over his own "O... Saya" (co-written with M.I.A.). Resul Pookutty's sound-mixing win, shared with his colleagues, made him one of the first Indians honored in that category.

## Production and Reception

Boyle chose Rahman for the score in part for his fusion of Indian classical music with R&B, hip-hop, and house influences; Rahman reportedly completed much of the work in a matter of weeks. The film had already swept major precursor awards, winning four Golden Globes and seven BAFTAs, establishing it as the clear front-runner entering Oscar night. Its success was widely framed as a breakthrough moment for Indian cinema's visibility on the global stage, even as some critics in India debated its portrayal of urban poverty.

## Significance

The film's near-sweep at the 2009 ceremony underscored a broader internationalization of the Academy Awards and demonstrated that a low-budget, multilingual production with a largely unknown cast could capture Hollywood's top prize. A. R. Rahman's double win brought unprecedented mainstream Western recognition to an Indian composer, and the victory of "Jai Ho" turned the song into a global hit. *Slumdog Millionaire* remains a landmark example of crossover cinema and one of the defining cultural successes of 2009.
