# Death of Michael Jackson (June 25, 2009)

**Category**: Arts & Culture
**Key figures**: Michael Jackson (entertainer), Conrad Murray (personal physician), AEG Live (concert promoter), Los Angeles County Coroner

## Summary

Michael Jackson, widely regarded as one of the most influential entertainers of the twentieth century, died on June 25, 2009, at Ronald Reagan UCLA Medical Center in Westwood, Los Angeles, California. He was 50 years old. Emergency services were called to his rented home at 100 North Carolwood Drive in Holmby Hills at approximately 12:21 p.m. Pacific Daylight Time; Jackson was pronounced dead at 2:26 p.m. PDT.

At the time of his death, Jackson had been preparing for a large-scale comeback concert series titled "This Is It," comprising 50 shows scheduled at London's O2 Arena from July 2009. Concert promoter AEG Live had hired Dr. Conrad Murray as Jackson's personal physician to oversee his health during the rehearsal and tour period. Jackson had been experiencing chronic insomnia, and Murray had been administering propofol — a powerful surgical anesthetic — intravenously each night to help Jackson sleep, a practice outside any recognized medical standard of care.

In the early hours of June 25, Murray administered propofol along with the benzodiazepines lorazepam and midazolam to Jackson. Murray left Jackson's bedside briefly and returned to find him not breathing. Murray performed CPR and attempted resuscitation, but did not call emergency services for an extended period — prosecutors later established the gap at approximately 90 minutes. The Los Angeles County Coroner ruled Jackson's death acute propofol intoxication and classified it as a homicide. In February 2010, Los Angeles County charged Murray with involuntary manslaughter. His trial began in September 2011, and on November 7, 2011, a jury found him guilty. Murray received a four-year prison sentence; he was released in October 2013 after serving approximately two years.

The global reaction to Jackson's death was immediate and unprecedented in scale. The announcement crashed multiple major websites, including TMZ (which first reported the death), Google, Twitter, and Wikipedia; Twitter recorded approximately 5,000 tweets per minute mentioning Jackson within minutes of the news breaking. A public memorial service held on July 7, 2009, at the Staples Center in Los Angeles was attended by approximately 17,500 people, with an estimated global television audience of 2.5 billion. More than 1.2 million people applied for the lottery-distributed free tickets within 24 hours of availability.

## Significance

The death of Michael Jackson marked the end of a singular career that had redefined popular music, music video production, and live performance since the 1970s. Jackson's albums — particularly "Off the Wall" (1979), "Thriller" (1982, the best-selling album of all time), and "Bad" (1987) — established standards for production, cross-genre synthesis, and commercial reach that subsequent artists have consistently referenced. His choreographic innovations, including the moonwalk, became cultural touchstones recognized globally.

Jackson's death catalyzed an immediate reassessment of his cultural legacy. Critical retrospectives in major publications across the world emphasized the depth and breadth of his influence across genres from rhythm and blues to rock to dance pop. Posthumous releases, including "This Is It" (a documentary and album drawn from rehearsal footage released in October 2009), demonstrated both the artistic ambitions of his planned return and the extent of his continued creative vitality. The documentary grossed over $260 million worldwide, becoming the highest-grossing concert film in history at that time.

The legal proceedings surrounding his death also had lasting significance for the entertainment industry. The Conrad Murray case focused public and regulatory attention on the abuse of anesthetic medications outside clinical settings and on the responsibilities of physicians serving celebrity clients under commercial pressure. The trial drew scrutiny to the relationship between concert promoters, personal medical staff, and the physical risks facing high-profile performers. Jackson's estate, which was deeply in debt at the time of his death, subsequently recovered through posthumous releases and licensing to become one of the most financially productive in entertainment history.
