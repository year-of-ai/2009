**Key figures**: Tiger Woods, Elin Nordegren

## Summary

In the early hours of November 27, 2009, golfer Tiger Woods crashed his Cadillac Escalade into a fire hydrant and a tree while pulling out of the driveway of his home in the gated Isleworth community near Windermere, Florida. His wife, Elin Nordegren, used a golf club to break out the vehicle's rear window and pull Woods from the SUV; he was treated for facial lacerations at a nearby hospital and released. The Florida Highway Patrol investigated the single-vehicle crash, found no evidence that alcohol was involved, and cited Woods for careless driving, a non-criminal infraction carrying a $164 fine and four points on his license.

The crash prompted intense media scrutiny that, over the following days, surfaced allegations of multiple extramarital affairs. Woods initially described the crash and ensuing coverage as a private matter but on December 2, 2009 issued a statement acknowledging "transgressions" and apologizing to his family, and further tabloid and news reports naming additional women followed through early December. On December 11, 2009, Woods posted a statement on his website announcing he was taking an "indefinite break from professional golf," writing that he needed to focus on being "a better husband, father and person" and, for the first time, using the word "infidelity" to describe his conduct.

The scandal quickly affected Woods's endorsement portfolio. Accenture, which had built advertising campaigns around Woods, ended its sponsorship on December 13, 2009, stating he was no longer the right representative for the company given the preceding weeks' events. AT&T followed on December 31, 2009, announcing it was ending its sponsorship agreement and wishing Woods well. Other sponsors, including Gillette, scaled back or phased out their association with Woods around the same time, while Gatorade discontinued its "Gatorade Tiger" product; Nike was among the sponsors that retained its relationship with him.

## Significance

The scandal marked an abrupt fall for the world's most commercially valuable athlete, whose earnings and global marketability had made him the face of golf and one of the highest-paid endorsers in sports. The rapid, public withdrawal of corporate sponsors such as Accenture and AT&T within weeks of the crash illustrated how quickly companies could sever ties with an athlete once a private matter became a sustained media story, and became a widely cited case study in sports-marketing and crisis-communications circles. Researchers later estimated that shareholders of Woods's sponsor companies collectively lost billions of dollars in market value in the aftermath.

The intensity and speed of the coverage, amplified by the still-emerging role of social media and cable news in celebrity reporting, also reflected a shift in how personal scandals involving public figures unfolded and spread. Woods's absence from competition affected PGA Tour television ratings and tournament attention through the following season, underscoring his outsized importance to golf's broader visibility and commercial appeal.
