## Summary

India held its 15th Lok Sabha (lower house) general election across five phases from April 16 to May 13, 2009, with results declared on May 16. Nearly 717 million citizens were registered to vote — the largest electorate in the world at that time — and an estimated 417 million ballots were cast, yielding a turnout of approximately 58% (58.21%). The Indian National Congress (INC) secured a decisive and unexpected victory under Prime Minister Manmohan Singh, and the Congress-led United Progressive Alliance (UPA) coalition won 262 of 543 Lok Sabha seats — just short of the 272 needed for an outright majority, but close enough to form a stable government with the support of a handful of smaller parties and far ahead of the opposition. Manmohan Singh was sworn in for his second consecutive term on May 22, 2009, becoming the first prime minister since Jawaharlal Nehru in 1962 to be re-elected after completing a full five-year term.

Pre-election polling had broadly predicted Congress would fall short of its 2004 seat total, requiring a stronger coalition with regional parties to govern. Instead, Congress alone won 206 seats — up from 145 in 2004, its largest parliamentary gain in nearly two decades — confounding pollsters and projecting the INC back to parliamentary dominance not seen since the 1980s. Voter participation from rural constituencies and women voters was notably high, and the Election Commission deployed more than 828,000 polling stations across 28 states and 7 union territories, using Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs) throughout.

The chief opposition, the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP)-led National Democratic Alliance (NDA), won only 159 seats combined, with the BJP itself reduced to 116 seats — a significant contraction from its 138-seat 2004 performance. BJP's campaign had positioned its leader L.K. Advani as "Prime Minister in waiting," but voters rejected this framing. Regional parties remained powerful in specific states: the All India Anna Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam (AIADMK) and the Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam (DMK) dominated Tamil Nadu; the Trinamool Congress made gains in West Bengal; the Biju Janata Dal (BJD) held Odisha; and the Samajwadi Party (SP) and Bahujan Samaj Party (BSP) competed fiercely for Uttar Pradesh's 80 seats.

## The Five Phases

India's election was administered over five sequential phases to allow security forces to be redeployed across the country between rounds:

| Phase | Date | States/Regions | Seats Contested |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | April 16 | Andhra Pradesh (part), Sikkim, parts of Assam, Jammu & Kashmir, Nagaland, Arunachal Pradesh | ~124 |
| 2 | April 22–23 | Uttar Pradesh (part), Rajasthan, Andhra Pradesh (more), Gujarat, Goa, parts of Bihar | ~141 |
| 3 | April 30 | Uttar Pradesh (part), West Bengal, Madhya Pradesh, Bihar (part), Assam (part), Manipur | ~107 |
| 4 | May 7 | Maharashtra, Uttar Pradesh (part), Jharkhand, West Bengal (part), Odisha, Himachal Pradesh | ~85 |
| 5 | May 13 | Punjab, Haryana, Uttarakhand, Delhi NCT, Uttar Pradesh (remainder), West Bengal (remainder), Bihar (remainder), Rajasthan (remainder) | ~86 |

Counting took place simultaneously across the country on May 16; results were substantially complete by late afternoon of that day. The phased structure, a logistical standard since the 1990s due to India's geographic scale and the demands of election security in conflict-affected areas (Naxalite regions, Jammu & Kashmir), required the central government and the Election Commission to coordinate approximately 6 million election officials and security personnel.

## Electoral Outcome

The decisive outcome gave the UPA coalition — Congress plus smaller allied parties including the Nationalist Congress Party (NCP), the DMK, and the Rashtriya Janata Dal — enough seats to govern without seeking support from the Left parties (the CPM-led Left Front), which had withdrawn UPA support in 2008 over the Indo-US civil nuclear deal and had pushed early elections. Freed of Left Front dependence, Singh's second government could advance economic liberalization, the nuclear cooperation framework, and market-friendly reforms with a broader parliamentary base.

Key seat tallies from the final result:

| Party / Alliance | 2004 seats | 2009 seats | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Indian National Congress (INC) | 145 | 206 | +61 |
| UPA coalition total | ~222 | 262 | +40 |
| Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) | 138 | 116 | −22 |
| NDA coalition total | ~186 | 159 | −27 |
| Left Front (CPI-M, CPI et al.) | 59 | 24 | −35 |
| BSP | 19 | 21 | +2 |
| SP | 36 | 23 | −13 |

The Left Front's collapse — from 59 to 24 seats — was the election's other major surprise, and reflected voter dissatisfaction with Left-governed West Bengal and a national rejection of the Left's obstructionism on the nuclear deal.

## Aftermath

Manmohan Singh's second government (UPA-II) inherited a moderately recovering economy. India's GDP growth had dipped to approximately 6.7% in 2008–09 amid the global financial crisis — slower than the prior three years but dramatically stronger than most developed economies. The new government pushed forward with the Unique Identification Authority of India (UIDAI), later Aadhaar, and accelerated infrastructure investment under the National Infrastructure Pipeline concept.

Internationally, the Congress victory reinforced the 2008 Indo-US civil nuclear deal — signed under UPA-I — giving it legislative stability through the full implementation of 123 Agreement safeguards and enabling India's entry into the Nuclear Suppliers Group (NSG) waiver framework. The Obama administration's diplomatic team, taking office the same year, saw the Singh re-election as a strategic asset for US–India relations (see [Barack Obama's Nobel Peace Prize]({{ '/news/history-politics/obama-nobel-peace-prize-2009/' | relative_url }}) for broader 2009 US diplomatic context).

The BJP's losses prompted a protracted internal leadership debate; it eventually led to the rise of Narendra Modi — at the time Chief Minister of Gujarat — as a national figure, foreshadowing the 2014 BJP landslide. The 2009 result is therefore often read as the high-water mark of Congress dominance before its structural decline.

## Significance

The 2009 election affirmed India's democratic stability during global economic turmoil and validated the EVM-based election infrastructure that had been rolled out progressively since 1999. Manmohan Singh's re-election enabled continuity in India's economic reform trajectory: the rupee, equity markets, and foreign direct investment had weathered 2008's crisis better than many peer markets, and voters rewarded the incumbent coalition.

The Congress victory also demonstrated the limits of majoritarian Hindu-nationalist mobilization in a diverse electorate, and demonstrated that coalition arithmetic could not substitute for governance performance. Regionally, Congress's gains in Andhra Pradesh, Maharashtra, and Rajasthan, combined with the Left's collapse in West Bengal, showed that incumbency punishment was geographically selective, applying to regional parties that had visibly underperformed.

India's 2009 diplomatic positioning — at [COP15 in Copenhagen]({{ '/news/history-politics/cop15-copenhagen-2009/' | relative_url }}) eight months later, India would drive a group of large emerging economies (the BASIC bloc: Brazil, South Africa, India, China) to negotiate the Copenhagen Accord outside the formal UNFCCC process — reflected the confidence of a re-elected government with a clear parliamentary mandate.

The [American Recovery and Reinvestment Act]({{ '/news/society-economics/american-recovery-act-2009/' | relative_url }}), signed in February 2009, and India's own domestic stimulus (the two fiscal packages announced in December 2008 and January 2009) illustrate the parallel democratic responses to the global recession that defined the 2009 political year across major democracies.
